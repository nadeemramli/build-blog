# Retention

> [!summary]
> The third Distribution phase — keeping customers and compounding their value. Three mechanisms: **product stickiness**, the **customer database**, and **community**. Retention is the most under-instrumented and highest-value part of most businesses.

## The three retention mechanisms

### 1. Product stickiness
The product itself pulls people back — through habit, results, or switching cost. The most durable form of retention because it needs no ongoing spend.
- Course business: curriculum that builds, certifications, updated content.
- Peptide / consumable: re-order cycles, subscriptions.

### 2. Customer database
The list you own — emails, contacts — grown and worked through **email flows**. This is the asset that survives platform changes, so it's a genuine **moat**.
- Fed by [[Conversion and Engagement#Website|website]] captures and purchases.
- Worked by lifecycle email (welcome, nurture, win-back).

### 3. Community
Retention through belonging — a group, server, or membership where customers stay for each other, not just the product.

## Why retention deserves more attention than it gets

> [!tip] Retention is where the money compounds
> Acquisition gets the glamour, but **LTV is set by retention**, and LTV is what licenses you to spend on acquisition. The single most valuable instrument to build is a **cohort retention curve** (what % of each month's customers are still active N months later). It's also the most commonly missing one.

## Automation notes — and a deliberate limit

| Mechanism | Automation stance |
|---|---|
| Customer database / email flows | **High automation** (BAU) — lifecycle emails, segmentation, win-back triggers |
| Product stickiness | Mostly product work, not automation |
| Community | **Automate logistics, keep humans on relationships** |

> [!warning] Don't over-automate community
> The original example is exactly right: *"updating a user when they come into the community"* is **BAU** (automate the welcome/onboarding logistics). But the *relationship* work — answering, recognizing, moderating tone — is what makes a community sticky, and automating it away kills the thing you're retaining people with. Automate the doorman, not the friendship. See [[Part B2 - BAU vs Pilot Framework#Worked examples]].

## KPIs
- Repeat purchase rate, subscription churn
- Cohort retention % at 1/3/6 months
- LTV, LTV:CAC ratio
- Community: active-member %, posts per member
